SCBF FOUNDATIONAL TRAUMA TRAINING FRAMEWORK
============================================================

When working with traumatized youth, the school environment must be sensitively adapted to provide a safe, predictable and nurturing environment. The core shift in a trauma-informed approach is from asking "What is wrong with this student?" to asking "What happened to this student?" This single reframe changes everything about how educators respond to behavior, build relationships, and design support.

INTRODUCTION

A trauma-informed school environment is one in which all students feel safe, supported, and nurtured, allowing them to thrive academically and socially. Children who have experienced trauma often face unique challenges in their educational journey, and without appropriate support, these challenges can become barriers to their success.

Trauma can significantly influence a child's cognitive development, behavior, and ability to form relationships. It can result in difficulty concentrating, emotional outbursts, withdrawal from peers, and a myriad of other issues that interfere with their learning and social development.

Creating a trauma-informed school environment does not just benefit students who have experienced trauma -- it benefits all students. A trauma-informed approach fosters a safe, compassionate, and engaging learning environment that nurtures the emotional well-being of all students.

UNDERSTANDING TRAUMA IN CHILDREN

Trauma refers to an emotional response to a distressing or life-threatening event, such as abuse, neglect, natural disasters, or loss. It can disrupt a child's sense of safety and well-being and have long-lasting effects on their physical, emotional, and cognitive development.

Types of trauma include interpersonal trauma (physical or sexual abuse), community violence, natural disasters, medical trauma, and complex trauma resulting from chronic exposure to multiple traumatic events. Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) -- including abuse, neglect, household dysfunction, parental separation, domestic violence, and household substance abuse -- are strongly correlated with negative outcomes in learning, behavior, and long-term health.

Children who have experienced trauma may exhibit increased agitation, withdrawal, aggression, or mood swings. They may struggle with concentration or show a decrease in academic performance. Some children show physical symptoms such as unexplained aches and pains, changes in appetite, or difficulties with sleep. It is essential to understand that these are often the child's way of coping with traumatic experiences -- not willful disobedience or defiance.

CREATING A SAFE AND INCLUSIVE ENVIRONMENT

A crucial element of a trauma-informed school is creating an environment where students feel safe, both physically and emotionally. This involves maintaining an orderly, predictable, and welcoming physical environment.

A sense of belonging is also vital. Schools can foster this by developing a culture that celebrates diversity and encourages inclusive, respectful interactions. A code of conduct that prioritizes respect, kindness, understanding, and empathy should be prominently displayed and consistently reinforced.

- Physical safety: Ensure the school environment feels safe -- organized, welcoming, with sensory tools like stress balls or fidget toys available
- Emotional safety: Establish a classroom culture that prioritizes empathy, respect, and open communication
- Belonging: Foster inclusivity through morning check-ins, restorative circles, and visible celebration of diversity

STAFF TRAUMA-INFORMED TRAINING

All staff members -- including teachers, administrative staff, and maintenance staff -- need to understand the impact of trauma on a child's learning and behavior. Training should focus on recognizing signs of trauma, understanding how trauma affects behavior, and strategies for responding in a supportive and effective manner. This approach ensures a uniform, school-wide understanding and supportive response to trauma-related behaviors.

FLEXIBLE RULES AND EXPECTATIONS

Children who have experienced trauma may have difficulty with rules and expectations that other students find easy to navigate. This is not because these children are inherently defiant, but because trauma can significantly impact a child's ability to focus, follow directions, and manage emotions.

Flexibility does not mean letting traumatized children get away with inappropriate behavior. It means understanding the context of their behavior, giving them the support they need to succeed, and implementing reasonable accommodations where necessary. For example, a child with a trauma history who becomes extremely anxious during tests may need extended time, a quieter environment, or breaks.

- Adapt rules to accommodate individual circumstances
- Communicate expectations clearly and in a trauma-sensitive manner
- Focus on encouraging positive behavior rather than only addressing negative behavior

SOCIAL AND EMOTIONAL LEARNING (SEL)

Trauma can significantly impact a child's ability to understand and regulate their emotions and to form and maintain relationships with peers. Integrating SEL into the curriculum can help children develop these vital skills.

SEL covers self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, and responsible decision-making. These skills should not be taught in isolation but integrated across all areas of the curriculum and modeled by staff throughout the school day.

- Teach emotional regulation and coping skills explicitly
- Promote resilience and empathy through activities and discussions
- Integrate SEL into all subject areas, not just dedicated SEL time

MINDFULNESS AND RELAXATION TECHNIQUES

Children who have experienced trauma often live in a state of heightened arousal or "fight, flight, or freeze" mode. Mindfulness and relaxation techniques can help these children calm their minds and bodies, allowing them to be present and engaged.

These techniques should be integrated into the daily routine for all students -- normalizing them and teaching valuable skills for managing stress. Examples include guided breathing exercises, yoga, progressive muscle relaxation, body scans, and guided visualizations.

ART AND PLAY THERAPY

Art and play are natural forms of expression for children, making them particularly useful tools in a trauma-informed approach. These mediums allow children to express their feelings, fears, and experiences indirectly when they might struggle to put them into words.

Art and play therapy in the classroom can take many forms: drawing, painting, role-playing, and using toys or games. Activities should be carried out in a safe and supportive environment, allowing the child to explore and express at their own pace. Educators should approach these activities with an open, non-judgmental mindset.

When a student is non-compliant or disengaged from traditional classwork, expressive arts programming is the SCBF preferred alternative. Offering art, music, drama, creative writing, or movement as alternatives provides increased engagement, emotional expression and processing, enhanced self-esteem, and differentiated learning.

INDIVIDUAL COUNSELING

For some children, individual counseling may be necessary to help them process their traumatic experiences and develop coping strategies. If resources allow, having a school counselor trained in trauma-informed care can be incredibly beneficial. Counseling should be available to students who show significant signs of distress or whose trauma-related behaviors are severely impacting their learning or social interactions. Consent should always be obtained from the child and their caregivers before counseling is initiated.

PEER SUPPORT GROUPS

Peer support groups can be an effective way to help children who have experienced trauma feel less alone in their experiences. Facilitated by a trained adult, these groups provide a safe space for children to share their feelings, learn from others who have had similar experiences, and develop supportive relationships. These groups must be carefully managed to ensure clear guidelines for respectful and confidential communication.

CRISIS MANAGEMENT

Even with the most comprehensive support in place, crisis situations may arise. A pre-prepared crisis management plan should clearly define what constitutes a crisis, the roles and responsibilities of staff members, and steps to de-escalate the situation. Staff should be trained in trauma-sensitive de-escalation techniques and understand the importance of maintaining a calm, understanding demeanor during these challenging situations.

COMMUNICATION WITH PARENTS AND GUARDIANS

Parents and guardians play a crucial role in supporting children who have experienced trauma. Regular communication is essential for understanding a child's experiences and coordinating support strategies at school and home. When communicating with parents or guardians, approach conversations with empathy, understanding, and respect, being sensitive to their own potential trauma histories or stressors.

Calling home to share positive behaviors and accomplishments has powerful benefits that are often underutilized:
- Strengthens relationships between educators, students, and families
- Encourages increased parental involvement
- Boosts student confidence and motivation
- Provides positive reinforcement that shapes future behavior
- Enhances classroom climate and builds trust
- Creates a supportive network where families and school work together

By consistently making positive calls home, educators celebrate accomplishments, strengthen relationships, and create a collaborative environment where students can thrive.

SELF-CARE FOR STAFF

Working with children who have experienced trauma can be emotionally challenging. Vicarious trauma -- absorbing the emotional weight of students' experiences -- and compassion fatigue -- the gradual erosion of empathy from sustained exposure to suffering -- are recognized occupational hazards of this work, not personal failures.

Prioritizing self-care is not just important for individual staff members but for the overall effectiveness of a trauma-informed approach. Staff should be encouraged to maintain physical health, take breaks, seek support from colleagues, and engage in activities they enjoy outside of work. Support should be available for staff to process their feelings, including regular debriefing sessions or access to counseling services.

CONFIDENTIALITY AND REPORTING

Maintaining confidentiality is a crucial aspect of a trauma-informed approach. Students and their families need to trust that their personal information will be handled sensitively. However, confidentiality does not extend to situations where a child is at risk of harm. Staff must understand their legal and ethical obligations to report suspicions or disclosures of abuse or neglect to the appropriate authorities.
