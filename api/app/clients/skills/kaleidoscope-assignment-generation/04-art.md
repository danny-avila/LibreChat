============================================================
ART ASSIGNMENTS
============================================================

Visual art assignments give students a non-verbal expressive channel -- particularly valuable for students who struggle to articulate their internal world in words. All art assignments should offer choice of medium wherever possible.

------------------------------------------------------------
FUTURISTIC CITY SCULPTURE
------------------------------------------------------------
Category: Art
Grade Range: Grades 4-12
Activity Type: Standard
Physical Space Required: Desk or table space with room for materials. Surface protection recommended.
Physical Limitations: Fine motor difficulties may require modified sculpting materials (larger clay pieces, found object assembly rather than fine detail work). Offer alternative 2D design option for students with significant motor limitations.
Therapeutic Goals: Primary: Hope and future orientation | Secondary: Choice and autonomy; Mastery and accomplishment; Improving focus and attention

Therapeutic Framing:
Building a future city is an act of hope -- the student is literally constructing a world they believe is possible. For students whose present feels unsafe or hopeless, imagining and building a better future is a direct therapeutic intervention. The Technology component invites the student to imagine solutions to problems -- a fundamentally optimistic cognitive act. The Cultural Identity component asks: what do the people of this future value? This question surfaces the student's own values and aspirations without making them the subject. The Interaction component -- how do people relate to this sculpture? -- subtly asks the student to imagine safe, purposeful human connection. The three-dimensional nature of sculpture is important: students are not just describing a future, they are physically constructing it.

Description:
In this activity, you will create a sculpture representing a building, monument, or public space in an imaginary futuristic city. This city has its own culture, advanced technology, and unique identity -- and your sculpture is a window into it.

Student-Facing Components:
- Architecture: Design a unique architectural feature for your sculpture -- what shapes, forms, and materials define it?
- Technology: Incorporate an element of advanced technology -- how does it enhance the space or the people who use it?
- Cultural Identity: Reflect something about the city's values or culture in the design
- Interaction: How would people in this city use or relate to your sculpture? Is it a gathering place, a landmark, something else?

Student Instructions:
1. Choose your sculpting medium (clay, paper mache, wire, cardboard, found objects, or a combination)
2. Sketch your ideas before building if that helps you plan
3. Build your sculpture with attention to all four components
4. Be prepared to share what your sculpture represents and why you made the choices you did

ENCHANTED FOREST CREATURE
------------------------------------------------------------
Category: Art
Grade Range: Grades 3-12
Activity Type: Standard
Physical Space Required: Desk or table space with room for art materials
Physical Limitations: Offer a range of mediums to accommodate fine motor differences. Younger students may benefit from pre-drawn body templates to build upon.
Therapeutic Goals: Primary: Self-identity and self-worth | Secondary: Recognizing personal strengths; Empathy and perspective-taking; Processing trauma indirectly

Therapeutic Framing:
Designing a creature that belongs to an enchanted ecosystem invites the student to explore identity, role, and belonging without autobiographical pressure. The Abilities component is particularly therapeutically rich -- what magical powers would this creature have? Students almost always choose abilities that reflect something they need or wish they possessed. The Role in the Ecosystem component asks: what is this creature's purpose? What does it contribute? This is a profound therapeutic question for students who struggle with self-worth and feel like a burden. The Legend component -- what stories are told about this creature -- allows students to imagine how they would be remembered or perceived if seen truly. The connection to nature and ecosystem also grounds the activity in the SCBF principle that all living things are interconnected and have inherent value.

Description:
In this activity, you will create an art piece featuring a creature that lives in an enchanted forest. Your creature is entirely unique -- it has its own appearance, abilities, habitat, and role in the ecosystem it belongs to.

Student-Facing Components:
- Appearance: What does your creature look like? Describe and depict its colors, size, and distinguishing features
- Abilities: What magical or extraordinary abilities does your creature have? How do they help it survive or contribute to the forest?
- Habitat: Where does your creature live within the enchanted forest? How does it interact with its surroundings?
- Role in the Ecosystem: What is your creature's purpose? How does it interact with other creatures and maintain balance?
- Legend: Is there a story or myth associated with your creature that the other forest inhabitants tell?

Student Instructions:
1. Choose your art medium (drawing, painting, sculpture, collage, digital art -- whatever feels right)
2. Develop all five components to create a fully realized creature with a genuine place in its world
3. Use color, texture, and detail to bring your creature to life
4. Think about what makes your creature special -- not just what it looks like but what it means

------------------------------------------------------------
MYSTERIOUS DOOR DESIGN
------------------------------------------------------------
Category: Art
Grade Range: Grades 3-12
Activity Type: Standard
Physical Space Required: Desk or table space with room for art materials
Physical Limitations: Offer a range of mediums to accommodate fine motor differences. Digital design tools are an appropriate alternative.
Therapeutic Goals: Primary: Emotional expression | Secondary: Processing trauma indirectly; Self-identity and self-worth; Hope and future orientation

Therapeutic Framing:
The door is one of the most powerful metaphors in therapeutic work -- it represents transition, threshold, choice, and the boundary between what is known and unknown. For traumatized students, a door can represent the boundary between the past and a possible future, between the unsafe and the safe, or between who they have been and who they might become. The Backstory component often surfaces narrative about origins and history. The Functionality component -- where does this door lead? -- is where the student projects hope, fear, or longing about what lies ahead. The Personal Connection component is written last intentionally, after the student has built creative distance through the fictional object. This sequencing allows students to arrive at personal meaning rather than being pushed toward it. Never force the personal connection -- for many students the fictional layer is sufficient and valuable.

Description:
In this activity, you will create an art piece featuring a mysterious door with a unique design, a history, and a special purpose. Where it leads, who made it, and what it means are entirely up to you.

Student-Facing Components:
- Description: Design your door -- what does it look like? What color, material, symbols, or patterns does it have?
- Backstory: What is the history of this door? Who built it, and what has it been used for?
- Functionality: Where does your door lead? What or who is on the other side? Does it have any special properties?
- Role in the Story: Who is connected to this door? What is their relationship with it?
- Personal Connection: Does this door remind you of anything in your own life -- a feeling, a choice, a place? (This part is optional and private if you prefer)

Student Instructions:
1. Choose your art medium (drawing, painting, mixed media, collage, or digital)
2. Make your door feel like it has a real history -- let the design tell a story
3. Develop all components to give your door a complete identity
4. The personal connection component is always optional -- share only what feels comfortable
