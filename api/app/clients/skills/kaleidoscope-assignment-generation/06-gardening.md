============================================================
GARDENING ASSIGNMENTS
============================================================

Gardening assignments use the relationship between student and living plant as a therapeutic vehicle. These assignments work best as ongoing activities woven through a program rather than single sessions.

------------------------------------------------------------
PLANT BUDDY PROJECT
------------------------------------------------------------
Category: Gardening
Grade Range: Grades K-12
Activity Type: Standard (ongoing -- recommended 4-12 weeks)
Physical Space Required: A designated space for the plant -- windowsill, desk corner, or outdoor plot. Plant and basic care supplies required.
Physical Limitations: Adapt plant selection and care tasks for students with mobility or fine motor limitations. Watering with a small cup, observing and journaling, or verbal documentation are all valid participation forms.
Therapeutic Goals: Primary: Responsibility and follow-through | Secondary: Building trust with adults; Empathy and perspective-taking; Resilience and coping; Self-identity and self-worth

Therapeutic Framing:
The Plant Buddy Project is the most relationally rich gardening assignment because it establishes an ongoing bond between student and living thing. For students who have experienced inconsistent or harmful caregiving, being the reliable caregiver for another living thing is a corrective experience -- they become the source of safety and nurturing they may not have reliably received. The student's relationship with the plant also mirrors the educator-student relationship: trust, attention, and consistency produce growth. The Reflection and Life Lessons component is where the therapeutic depth lies -- students who are ready make connections between the plant's experience and their own. Do not prompt these connections too directly; let them emerge. The ongoing nature of this assignment (weeks, not days) is essential to its therapeutic power -- the relationship with the plant must have time to become real.

Description:
In this ongoing activity, you will choose a plant to be your plant buddy and care for it throughout the program. You will observe its growth, document your observations, and reflect on what you learn from this unique relationship.

Student-Facing Components:
- Plant Buddy Selection: Choose a plant that you feel drawn to. Consider its qualities and what it might teach you
- Care and Nurturing: Learn what your plant needs and provide it consistently -- water, light, pruning, attention
- Observation and Documentation: Regularly observe and document your plant's growth, changes, and challenges through sketches, photographs, or written notes in a plant buddy journal
- Reflection and Life Lessons: Reflect on what you learn from caring for your plant. How does your plant's experience connect to your own life?

Student Instructions:
1. Choose a plant that genuinely interests you -- you will be caring for it for a long time
2. Research what your specific plant needs and commit to providing it consistently
3. Keep your plant buddy journal updated -- observations do not need to be long, just honest
4. Let your reflections come naturally. There is no pressure to draw any particular connection

SENSORY GARDEN EXPLORATION
------------------------------------------------------------
Category: Gardening
Grade Range: Grades K-12
Activity Type: Standard (ongoing -- recommended 4-8 weeks)
Physical Space Required: A container garden or designated garden plot. Multiple plants with diverse sensory qualities required. Outdoor space preferred but indoor container gardening is viable.
Physical Limitations: Highly accessible. Students with allergies should avoid fragrant plants that may trigger reactions. Soil sensory sensitivity should be discussed in advance -- gloves are available.
Therapeutic Goals: Primary: Grounding and mindfulness | Secondary: Calming and immediate self-regulation; Body awareness and physical safety; Developing social skills (sharing component); Building self-regulation skills

Therapeutic Framing:
Sensory engagement is one of the most direct routes to present-moment grounding available in a school setting. The sensory garden deliberately activates multiple senses -- touch, smell, sight, and sometimes taste -- which anchors the student in the here-and-now and interrupts rumination or dissociation. The deliberate pairing of sensory experience with emotional reflection builds interoceptive awareness: students learn to notice what is happening in their bodies and to connect physical sensation to emotional state. The Garden Design component provides agency and choice. The ongoing daily engagement builds a practice of mindful attention that extends beyond the garden. This assignment is particularly effective for students who struggle with talk-based approaches to regulation.

Description:
In this activity, you will create and care for a small sensory garden filled with plants that engage different senses. You will spend time with your garden regularly and reflect on how the sensory experiences affect how you feel.

Student-Facing Components:
- Plant Selection: Choose a variety of plants that engage different senses -- fragrant herbs, soft textures, bright colors, interesting sounds in the wind
- Garden Design: Plan how to arrange your plants to create an inviting and calming space
- Planting and Care: Plant your selections and provide regular care
- Sensory Exploration and Reflection: Spend time with your garden daily. Touch, smell, observe. Record your emotional responses in a journal

Student Instructions:
1. Choose plants that genuinely appeal to your senses -- this garden is for you
2. When you visit your garden, take at least two minutes to be fully present with it before doing anything else
3. In your journal, try to be specific: not just "I felt calm" but "When I touched the lamb's ear it made me feel..."
4. Notice how your responses to the same plants change over time

MINDFUL PLANTING AND REFLECTION
------------------------------------------------------------
Category: Gardening
Grade Range: Grades 3-12
Activity Type: Standard (ongoing -- recommended 6-12 weeks)
Physical Space Required: Space for a planting container or garden plot. Soil, seeds or seedlings, and basic tools required.
Physical Limitations: Adapt physical tasks as needed. The reflective journaling component is the therapeutic core and accessible to all students.
Therapeutic Goals: Primary: Building self-regulation skills | Secondary: Grounding and mindfulness; Resilience and coping; Improving focus and attention; Calming and immediate self-regulation

Therapeutic Framing:
The deliberate pairing of physical planting with reflective journaling is what distinguishes this assignment from general gardening. The Mindful Planting component -- approaching the act of planting with full, intentional attention -- introduces mindfulness as a practical embodied experience rather than an abstract concept. Students who resist formal mindfulness exercises often engage readily with this because the plant gives the attention something concrete to rest on. The Reflective Journaling component is where the long-term therapeutic work happens: over weeks of journaling about the plant's growth and challenges, students inevitably write about their own. The parallel between plant growth and personal growth should be gently offered but never forced. The educator's most important role is consistency -- checking in regularly and treating the reflection as meaningful rather than a homework task.

Description:
In this ongoing activity, you will mindfully plant a seed or seedling and care for it over the course of the program. As your plant grows, you will reflect on your own growth, emotions, and the lessons you learn from caring for another living thing.

Student-Facing Components:
- Plant Selection: Choose a seed or seedling that resonates with you -- consider its growth habits and what it represents
- Mindful Planting: Plant your seed or seedling with full awareness and intention. Notice the soil, the delicacy of the seed, the care you put into creating a home for it
- Daily Care and Observation: Tend to your plant daily. Notice its growth, its challenges, its changes
- Reflective Journaling: Keep a journal throughout the program. Write about your experiences, what you observe, and any connections you notice between your plant's journey and your own

Student Instructions:
1. When you plant, do it slowly and deliberately -- notice everything you can about what you are doing
2. Care for your plant every day, even if just for a few minutes
3. In your journal, try to write at least a few sentences after each observation. What did you notice? What did it make you think or feel?
4. Let the connections between your plant and your own life come naturally -- you do not need to force them
