============================================================
MUSIC ASSIGNMENTS
============================================================

Music engages the emotional and embodied dimensions of experience in ways that bypass the verbal defenses traumatized students often use. No musical training is required for any assignment unless specifically noted.

------------------------------------------------------------
RHYTHM AND EMOTION EXPRESSION
------------------------------------------------------------
Category: Music
Grade Range: Grades K-12
Activity Type: Standard
Physical Space Required: Standard classroom space. Some movement involved -- students should have room to play their rhythm without restriction.
Physical Limitations: Adapt for students with limb differences or motor limitations -- vocal percussion, foot tapping, or body percussion are valid alternatives. Auditory sensitivities should be discussed in advance.
Therapeutic Goals: Primary: Emotional expression | Secondary: Anger and frustration release; Developing social skills (group version); Calming and immediate self-regulation

Therapeutic Framing:
Rhythm is one of the most direct routes to emotional regulation available in a classroom setting. Before language developed, humans used rhythm to synchronize emotional states and communicate feeling. For traumatized students who struggle to name or describe their emotions, creating a rhythm that expresses a feeling is often easier and more authentic than verbal expression. The physical act of playing a rhythm also provides safe discharge for states like anger, anxiety, or grief. The use of classroom objects rather than instruments deliberately lowers the threshold -- no musical skill is required and the familiar objects ground the activity. The Reflection component is important: it builds the bridge between the somatic experience and cognitive understanding. In group settings this assignment builds empathy and attunement as students experience each other's emotional rhythms.

Description:
In this activity, you will create a rhythm using objects around you that expresses a specific emotion. You will perform it for others and reflect on how rhythm and feeling connect.

Student-Facing Components:
- Emotion Selection: Choose an emotion you want to express through rhythm (any emotion is valid)
- Rhythm Creation: Create a unique rhythm using classroom objects -- consider how tempo, intensity, and pattern can carry the feeling
- Performance: Perform your rhythm, letting your body language and the way you play express the emotion
- Reflection: Write or share a few sentences about how you used rhythm to express your emotion and what you discovered

Student Instructions:
1. Choose an emotion that feels real to you right now or one you want to explore
2. Experiment with different objects and combinations to find the rhythm that fits your emotion
3. When you perform, let your whole body be part of the expression
4. In your reflection, think about: did the rhythm change how you felt while playing it?

MELODIC STORYTELLING
------------------------------------------------------------
Category: Music
Grade Range: Grades 3-12
Activity Type: Standard
Physical Space Required: Standard classroom space. Access to a musical instrument, voice recording device, or digital music tool required.
Physical Limitations: Vocal melody is accessible to most students. For students with vocal difficulties, digital music tools are an excellent alternative. Coordinate with educator on available technology.
Therapeutic Goals: Primary: Mastery and accomplishment | Secondary: Emotional expression; Self-identity and self-worth; Processing trauma indirectly

Therapeutic Framing:
Composing a melody is an act of creation that requires sustained focus, iterative problem-solving, and personal decision-making -- all of which build the executive function skills that trauma disrupts. The Artist Statement component is therapeutically significant: it asks the student to articulate the connection between their creative choices and their intended meaning, which is a metacognitive skill that transfers to emotional intelligence. The story or message behind the melody is where personal material often surfaces -- students frequently choose to tell their own stories through musical narrative when they would not tell them in words. The performance or recording creates a tangible artifact of accomplishment that the student can keep and return to. For students who have never created music before, the experience of composing something that genuinely moves others can be profoundly self-affirming.

Description:
In this activity, you will create a short melody that tells a story or carries a message. Using your voice, an instrument, or a digital tool, you will compose something that communicates through sound.

Student-Facing Components:
- Story or Message: Choose what your melody will express -- a personal experience, a fictional story, an emotion, or an idea
- Melody Creation: Compose a short melody (30-60 seconds) that musically represents your chosen story or message
- Performance or Recording: Perform your melody for others or create a recording
- Artist Statement: Write 3-5 sentences explaining what your melody is about and how you used musical elements to express it

Student Instructions:
1. Start with a feeling or image you want to create, then find the sounds that fit it
2. Experiment -- try different pitches, tempos, and rhythms until something feels right
3. Your melody does not need to be technically perfect. It needs to be true
4. Write your artist statement honestly -- explain what you were trying to say and whether you feel you said it

SOUND EXPLORATION AND EMOTION REFLECTION
------------------------------------------------------------
Category: Music
Grade Range: Grades K-12
Activity Type: Standard
Physical Space Required: Standard classroom space with access to a variety of objects and materials. A recording device or smartphone is needed.
Physical Limitations: Fully accessible to students with mobility limitations. Auditory processing differences should be discussed in advance.
Therapeutic Goals: Primary: Anger and frustration release | Secondary: Grounding and mindfulness; Emotional expression; Calming and immediate self-regulation

Therapeutic Framing:
Sound exploration activates present-moment attention -- to really hear a sound requires being here, now. This makes it a powerful grounding tool for students who dissociate or ruminate. The act of deliberately creating and then listening to a sound collage also gives students control over their sensory environment, which is therapeutic for students who have experienced environments they could not control. The Emotional Reflection component is the bridge between sensory experience and emotional literacy -- asking "why does this sound make me feel this way?" builds interoceptive awareness. Sharing and discussion in a group setting reveals that different people respond differently to the same sound, which is an important lesson in the subjectivity of emotional experience and the validity of one's own responses.

Description:
In this activity, you will explore how different sounds make you feel by creating a sound collage from objects around you and reflecting on your emotional responses.

Student-Facing Components:
- Sound Collection: Gather a variety of objects and materials that make interesting sounds
- Sound Collage Creation: Experiment with combinations of sounds and record your collage
- Emotional Reflection: Listen back to your collage and write 5-7 sentences about how each sound makes you feel and why you think it evokes those feelings
- Sharing and Discussion: Share your sound collage and reflection with others and listen to theirs

Student Instructions:
1. Collect materials that make very different kinds of sounds -- soft, sharp, rhythmic, random
2. Experiment freely before recording -- explore unexpected combinations
3. When you reflect, be honest about your emotional responses, even if they surprise you
4. In discussion, listen with curiosity -- other people may respond very differently to the same sounds
