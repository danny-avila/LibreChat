SECTION 1: THERAPEUTIC GOAL TAXONOMY
============================================================

Use this taxonomy to:
- Map educator language to therapeutic goals (behavioral signal mapping below)
- Select the primary goal driving assignment selection
- Generate new activities for GAP goals using the descriptions as your guide

BEHAVIORAL SIGNAL MAPPING -- educators rarely name goals directly:
- "Can't sit still" / "always anxious" / "panics easily" → Calming and immediate self-regulation
- "Zones out" / "seems far away" / "not present" → Grounding and mindfulness; Body awareness and physical safety
- "Shuts down" / "won't engage" / "refuses everything" → Building trust with adults; Emotional expression
- "Doesn't believe in herself" / "says she's stupid or worthless" → Self-identity and self-worth; Recognizing personal strengths
- "Blows up" / "explosive anger" / "can't control himself" → Anger and frustration release; Calming and immediate self-regulation
- "No friends" / "isolated" / "other kids avoid him" → Developing social skills; Sense of belonging and community
- "Can't finish anything" / "gives up immediately" → Responsibility and follow-through; Mastery and accomplishment
- "Seems hopeless" / "says nothing matters" / "no plans for the future" → Hope and future orientation; Resilience and coping
- "Doesn't know who she is" / "acts completely different with different people" → Self-identity and self-worth
- "Terrified of everything" / "doesn't feel safe anywhere" → Safety and trust in the environment; Grounding and mindfulness

CATEGORY 1: EMOTIONAL PROCESSING
------------------------------------------------------------

Emotional expression
Giving feelings a creative outlet -- allowing the student to externalize internal emotional states through art, writing, music, movement, or other expressive mediums without requiring direct verbal disclosure.

Processing trauma indirectly
Exploring difficult experiences through metaphor, fantasy, or fictional distance. The student works through real feelings without confronting trauma directly, using the safety of imagination as a buffer.

Grief and loss [GAP -- no current assignment, generate new activity]
Processing experiences of loss, significant change, endings, or absence -- whether the loss of a person, a relationship, a home, a sense of safety, or a version of themselves before trauma.

Anger and frustration release
Channeling intense emotions such as anger, frustration, or agitation constructively through physical or creative activity. Provides a safe, structured outlet that prevents internalization or destructive expression.

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY 2: REGULATION AND STABILITY
------------------------------------------------------------

Calming and immediate self-regulation
Reducing acute anxiety, agitation, or dysregulation in the present moment. Activities that activate the parasympathetic nervous system and bring the student back to a window of tolerance quickly.

Building self-regulation skills
Developing long-term capacity to recognize emotional states, use coping tools, and manage responses over time. Distinct from immediate calming -- this is about building durable skills through repeated practice.

Improving focus and attention
Strengthening the ability to sustain attention, follow multi-step instructions, and be present and engaged in an activity. Particularly relevant for students whose trauma manifests as hypervigilance or dissociation.

Grounding and mindfulness
Connecting to the present moment through physical senses, breath, or body awareness. Helps students who tend to dissociate, ruminate, or become overwhelmed by past or future thoughts.

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY 3: IDENTITY AND RESILIENCE
------------------------------------------------------------

Self-identity and self-worth
Exploring and affirming who the student is -- their values, preferences, personality, and unique qualities. Helps counter the identity disruption and shame that often accompanies trauma.

Recognizing personal strengths
Identifying and celebrating the student's capabilities, resilience qualities, and superpowers. Directly counters trauma-related self-perception that is limited to weakness, failure, or damage.

Resilience and coping
Building concrete tools and confidence to navigate difficulty. Activities that mirror the experience of overcoming challenges, caring through adversity, and discovering inner resources.

Hope and future orientation
Developing a positive sense of possibility, agency, and a future worth working toward. Particularly important for students whose trauma has made the future feel unsafe, unlikely, or irrelevant.

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY 4: RELATIONSHIPS AND CONNECTION
------------------------------------------------------------

Building trust with adults
Developing safety and openness with educators or counselors through low-pressure, collaborative activity. The activity itself becomes a shared experience that creates connection without requiring vulnerability.

Developing social skills
Practicing positive peer interaction, turn-taking, active listening, and communication in a structured, low-stakes context. Supports students whose trauma has disrupted peer relationship development.

Empathy and perspective-taking
Understanding and connecting with others' experiences, emotions, and viewpoints. Builds the capacity to see outside one's own trauma lens and relate to others with curiosity rather than threat.

Sense of belonging and community
Feeling part of a group or classroom. Activities with a sharing or collective component that help students experience themselves as valued members of a community rather than isolated or different.

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY 5: AGENCY AND MASTERY
------------------------------------------------------------

Responsibility and follow-through
Experiencing the satisfaction and self-respect that comes from caring for something over time. Builds executive function, routine, and the experience of being someone who follows through -- often disrupted by trauma.

Choice and autonomy
Making meaningful decisions and owning outcomes. Directly restores the sense of agency and control that trauma has taken away. Activities with genuine student choice built into their structure.

Mastery and accomplishment
Building genuine skill and confidence through real achievement. The student produces something they are proud of -- which may be the first time they have experienced this in an academic context.

Problem-solving
Developing the capacity to work through challenges independently, try different approaches, and persist. Activities that present structured challenges with multiple valid solutions.

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY 6: BODY AND PHYSICAL SAFETY
------------------------------------------------------------

Body awareness and physical safety
Developing awareness of physical sensations, recognizing the body's stress signals, and building a sense of safety within one's own body. Particularly important for students whose trauma has created disconnection from or fear of physical sensation.

Boundary setting and self-advocacy [GAP -- no current assignment, generate new activity]
Helping students recognize their personal physical and emotional limits, practice saying no, and develop language for communicating their needs safely to adults and peers. Builds the self-protective capacity that trauma often erodes.

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY 7: IDENTITY, CULTURE, AND COMMUNITY
------------------------------------------------------------

Cultural identity and heritage [GAP -- no current assignment, generate new activity]
Connecting students to their cultural background, family traditions, language, or community as a source of strength and identity. Particularly relevant for students who experience cultural disconnection at school or whose trauma intersects with cultural displacement.

Family and relationship mapping [GAP -- no current assignment, generate new activity]
Helping students safely explore their family systems, significant relationships, and attachment figures. Structured activities that allow students to understand and articulate who is in their world, what those relationships mean, and where they feel safe.

Safety and trust in the environment [GAP -- no current assignment, generate new activity]
Activities that help students identify safe people, safe places, and personal safety signals -- building an internal framework for recognizing and seeking safety. Particularly relevant for students who have experienced environmental instability or unpredictable caregiving.

------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY 8: JOY AND POSITIVE EXPERIENCE
------------------------------------------------------------

Celebration and joy [GAP -- no current assignment, generate new activity]
Activities designed to experience pleasure, play, and fun without a heavy therapeutic agenda. For students whose lives have had little room for joy, the experience of delight and lightness is itself deeply therapeutic -- it expands the emotional range beyond survival mode.
